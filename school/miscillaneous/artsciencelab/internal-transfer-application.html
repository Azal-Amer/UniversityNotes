  1. In your resume, you mentioned your role as an Undergraduate Research Assistant and Summer Fellow at the Quantum Computing Stream, Freshman Research Initiative at UT Austin. Can you elaborate on any specific projects, skills, or achievements from this experience that demonstrate your aptitude for computer science?
    In this class, I worked on an implementation of the QKD BB84 protocol, written in Python in collaboration with Anisha Baskar Torres (I dont know if the name is relevant here). After that, we ran an analysis of another team's implementation, in which we used two strategies. One was a brute force attack, which was optimized on our model. On our written model, I calculated the influence of a third party interference based on different possible snooping parameters, and then used a random forest algorithm on the data to see which parameters led to the highest captured data, with the lowest detection rate. After fitting a function to that, we then adjusted the initial parameters on the opponent's model given just the detection rate. Through this method, we could reliably steal 10% of the transferred private key without detection. The other method was analyzing their implementation, and recognizing that they actually threw out their private key, and kept their announced public key to use as a private key instead.
    Additionally in the class, for our final project me and my group worked to develop a website demoing the double slit experiment, written in reach. The site includes a visual demo of what a user can expect given the parameters, a visualization of the experimental setup given inputs, and a simulation mode allowing you to vary the number of photons used.

During my time in college, I have actively sought out opportunities to apply my knowledge and skills in practical settings through both academic research and personal projects. This semester, I had the privilege of participating in UT's Quantum Computing Stream under the guidance of Dr. Noah Davis. Due to the complexity of the subject matter, the first semester of the research group focused on learning techniques essential to being a valuable contributor in a quantum computing group. My partner and I were tasked with creating a top-down implementation of the QKD BB84 protocol. Additionally, we were given another team's implementation of the same protocol and asked to act as a third party attempting to evade detection while maximizing stolen information from the private key.

To accomplish this, we collected data from our personal implementation through parallel processing and used a random forest algorithm to determine the optimal parameters for the third party to maximize stolen information. Using this information, we designed an optimal strategy based on their protocol's detection rate. By fine-tuning the parameters, we successfully achieved a stolen information rate of 10% without detection.

Furthermore, as a final project, my team and I developed a website that models the wave nature of light through interference patterns. Built using React, the website allows users to interact with a diagram visualizing an experimental setup and observe its effect on the system. The website offers several modes, including graphs, a visualization of what a third-party observer would see, and a raw particle simulation, providing an engaging and educational experience for users.

Due to my dedication and performance in the Quantum Computing Stream during the semester, I was accepted into the group's summer fellowship, where I will have the opportunity to work part-time at the pickle research lab, a state-of-the-art facility dedicated to advancing the field of quantum computing.
